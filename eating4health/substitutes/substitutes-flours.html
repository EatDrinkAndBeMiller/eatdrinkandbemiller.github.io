Substituting Flour


Although the grocery store sells gluten-free flours that can replace all-purpose and wheat flours, many times these replacement flours contain ingredients that can spike your blood sugar (like starches) or ingredients that you can’t have due to the first phase or to food sensitivities. Be sure to check the food label for the ingredients.

Below are other gluten-free flours that are typically used in a gluten-free diet. To learn more about these flours, check out this resource or search “cooking with … (flour).”

These flours cannot replace all-purpose or wheat flour 1:1 in recipes. It is best to search for a recipe with the specific flour you want to use. Typically, these flours work best in combinations like buckwheat with brown rice flour or coconut with almond flour.

Because these flours lack gluten, many times a thickening agent like xanthan gum, glucomannan, psyllium husk, or a starch is added to the recipe. If you find a product falls apart easily, try replacing 1tsp of flour with one of these.

Gluten-free Flour Replacements:

  • Almond Flour
  • Coconut Flour
  • Garbanzo (or Chickpea) Flour
  • Brown Rice Flour
  • Ground Flaxseed
  • Quinoa Flour
  • Buckwheat Flour
  • Oat Flour

Almond Flour
Almond flour is the most common substitute for flour in baked goods. It can be substituted 1:1 for wheat flour in recipes. It’s recommended to add an additional egg as it will not rise like gluten-filled flour.

There is a difference between almond meal and almond flour. Almond flour is made from ground, blanched almonds, so it does not contain the skin. This is better for baking cookies or bread. Almond meal has a coarser texture and acts like breadcrumbs.

Storage: Keep in the fridge or the freezer. It stays fresh for up to 6 months in the FREEZER. Nuts can go rancid, so cold storage is best.

Too expensive? Try buying it in bulk at Sam’s Club or Costco. Another option is waiting for a sale on vitacost.com.

Coconut Flour
Coconut flour is made by drying coconut meat and grinding it into a flour. It will add a mild coconut flavor to baked goods.

It is good for baking breads and desserts. However, it is a “thirsty” flour and will require more water than regular flour or almond flour. If you use it to replace flour in your recipes, you will need to add additional liquid.

Where to find it? This popular flour is found at almost every grocery store. Natural Grocers carries organic for a fair price in their bulk section.

Garbanzo (Chickpea) Flour
Garbanzo, or chickpea, flour is made from ground dried chickpeas or garbanzo beans. It has a nutty taste and is popular in Middle Eastern and Indian cuisine.

This is a great source of fiber and plant-based protein. It is also high in magnesium and potassium.

Don’t know where to find it? Try Natural Grocer’s bulk items or online at vitacost.com.

Brown Rice Flour
Made from ground brown rice, it has nutty flavor. Brown rice flour works well for thickening sauces or making breaded foods like fish or chicken. It is also used to make noodles or combined with other gluten-free flours to make baked goods.

Not sure where to buy it? Brown rice flour can be found at Natural Grocers or online at vitacost.com. You can also make your own by grinding up dry brown rice.

Recipes for brown rice flour:
  • Brown Rice Flour Pancakes – Gluten Free - The Honour System (contains eggs; use a sweetener of choice)

Ground Flaxseed
Flaxseeds can be ground into a flour that is rich in fiber, omega-3s, and other nutrients. It has a nutty flavor and is rich in oils.

Ground flaxseed can be used to replace eggs, oil, or flour in a recipe, with some alterations to the recipes. Check out more about using ground flaxseed here.

It is recommended to use ground golden flaxseed in recipes because of its lighter flavor.

Try ground flaxseed with coconut flour:
  • Coconut Flour Low Carb Flax Bread or Muffins - Low Carb Yum

Quinoa Flour
Quinoa flour is made by grinding quinoa seeds; however, if you want to make this yourself, try using quinoa flakes from Natural Grocers. Quinoa seeds are very difficult to grind down in traditional kitchen appliances and require roasting first.

It works well in baked goods, except yeast bread, since it looks and feels like all-purpose flour. It can also be used as a thickening agent or added to smoothies and shakes like a protein powder.

With its earthy, nutty flavor, it is recommended to start in recipes with bold flavors like cocoa powder. It makes a great replacement for oat flour.

Recipes with quinoa flour:
  • Quinoa Flour Biscotti - Power Hungry (contains eggs; use a sweetener of choice)
  • Quinoa Flour Almond Flour Soda Bread - Power Hungry
  • Double Quinoa Power Cookies - Power Hungry (contains eggs; use a sweetener of choice)

Buckwheat Flour
Buckwheat flour comes from ground buckwheat groats. Although it contains the word “wheat,” it is not a wheat grain.

Buckwheat flour has a rich, earthy flavor and is great for quick and yeast breads. Because it lacks gluten, it will be crumbly. It is recommended to combine it with other gluten-free flours such as brown rice flour in order to produce the right consistency.

Try this buckwheat and brown rice flour bread:
  • Gluten-free Buckwheat Sandwich Bread - Power Hungry (note: psyllium husks can be found at Natural Grocers and vitacost.com.)

Oat Flour
Oat flour is made by grinding whole oats. Its flavor is closer to all-purpose flour and baked goods have a chewy and crumbly texture. It works well in baked goods.

Oat flour is very easy to make at home by simply grinding rolled oats in a food processor. Be sure to look for gluten-free oats as oats are easily contaminated with gluten in processing facilities.

Recipes with oat flour:

  • Healthy Peanut Butter Banana Muffins - Ambitious Kitchen (contains eggs)
  • Oat Flour Banana Bread - Confession of a Baking Queen (contains dairy)
